# Corn Seasonality

*Last reviewed: 2026-07-21 — provenance: verified calendar structure plus authored priors*

- March-May: U.S. planting pace, prevented planting, and acreage expectations
  become increasingly important.
- June-July: pollination weather, heat, and soil moisture can dominate yield
  uncertainty and nearby option volatility.
- July-August: grain fill, condition changes, and early yield estimates matter;
  new-crop December becomes the main production benchmark.
- September-November: harvest pace, realized yield, basis, storage capacity,
  and Grain Stocks evidence replace forecast uncertainty.
- December-February: South American weather, export competition, ethanol/feed
  demand, and U.S. acreage expectations become more prominent.

These are interpretation windows, not automatic directional signals. Compare
each observation with the same development stage and report week across years.
USDA's National Crop Progress and WASDE publications are the primary calendar
references.
