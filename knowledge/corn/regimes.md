# Corn Regime Framework

*Last reviewed: 2026-07-21 — provenance: authored priors, not yet calibrated*

Corn regimes should be described through four separate channels before they
are synthesized: the futures curve, crop supply, demand/export evidence, and
option/event pricing. Do not assign numerical historical bands until the Corn
deployment has accumulated measured ZC/OZC history.

- Nearby inverse spreads can indicate immediate scarcity, strong basis, or
  delivery-location demand. Carry can indicate storage and financing economics,
  but neither shape proves a cash-market arbitrage without basis and freight.
- Old-crop/new-crop spreads, especially July-December and September-December,
  bridge two different supply years and should not be interpreted like adjacent
  monthly carry.
- Falling good-plus-excellent crop condition can tighten yield expectations,
  but the effect depends on development stage, geography, weather forecasts,
  planted area, and prior yield assumptions.
- High option volatility around WASDE, Acreage, Grain Stocks, or pollination
  weather is event pricing. Direction comes from skew and the underlying curve,
  not from ATM volatility alone.
- Managed-money positioning can amplify weather or report surprises; it is not
  a fundamental supply estimate.

Mechanical `history_context` percentiles are the live comparison. Until they
mature, report exact values and observation counts without calling a two-day
high or low an extreme historical regime.
