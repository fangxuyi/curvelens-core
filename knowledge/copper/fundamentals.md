# Copper Physical Fundamentals

*Last reviewed: 2026-07-23 — provenance: verified official sources plus authored collection policy*

Copper analysis must keep the production chain explicit:

1. Mine output and ore grades determine concentrate availability.
2. Smelter capacity, outages, and treatment/refining charges determine the
   conversion bottleneck.
3. Refined production, scrap, imports/exports, and exchange or bonded stocks
   determine immediately available metal.
4. Fabrication, construction, grids, transport, machinery, and electronics
   determine consumption.

Do not collapse these stages into a generic “supply” signal. Falling treatment
charges can indicate concentrate tightness while refined inventory rises, and
headline electrification growth need not translate immediately into cathode
demand because project timing, copper intensity, substitution, and inventories
also matter.

The [USGS Copper statistics page](https://www.usgs.gov/centers/national-minerals-information-center/copper-statistics-and-information)
is the preferred official source for U.S. production, trade, consumption, and
annual summaries. USGS says public posting of its monthly Mineral Industry
Surveys is temporarily paused during a ScienceBase transition, so an old table
must never be presented as fresh daily evidence. Use the International Copper
Study Group, exchange warehouse reports, customs data, and producer filings
only with their publication date, covered period, geography, units, and
licensing status recorded.

China is central to global copper demand and processing, but the bootstrap
FRED series are U.S. or global proxies. A China conclusion requires a dated
China-specific source. Report exchange inventory by venue and location rather
than summing COMEX, LME, SHFE, and bonded stocks without checking definitions.
